What Does Sound Look Like?

Creating Media: Audio Recording  ·  Amazing Africa
Year 1 / Year 2 45–60 mins Whole class + pairs
Learning Objectives
All pupils

Know that sound can be seen as a wave on a screen and that the wave changes when the sound changes.

Most pupils

Record a short sound, see its waveform and play it back using the class app.

Some pupils

Begin to describe how they could change a recording to make the wave look and sound different.

Key Vocabulary This Lesson
sound wave digital microphone record playback volume data
Resources Needed
  • Audio clip with visible soundwave on smartboard — starting quiet and simple, building to loud and complex (cued up and tested before lesson)
  • SoundWave Studio on smartboard — hosted link open, microphone allowed, large red record button visible (same file as pupil iPads)
  • iPads — 1 per pair, SoundWave Studio bookmarked (?lesson=2 on the URL — full app with timeline and recordings)
  • Headphones — 1 pair per pupil (checked and working before lesson)
  • Talking stem on display: "The wave goes _______ when I _______."
  • File: soundwave-studio.html on your school web space or learning platform (must be opened via a web link, not from the desktop — otherwise the microphone will not work)
Lesson Sequence
Phase Teacher Activity & Pupil Task
Hook 5 mins smartboard
Watch the sound grow
Without saying anything, play the audio clip on the smartboard with the waveform visible. The clip starts quiet and simple — the wave is small and flat. As the sound builds in volume and complexity, pupils watch the wave grow, spike and fill the screen.

Let it play out. Then ask: "What did you notice happening to the picture as the sound got louder?" Take a few answers. Do not explain yet — just let them describe what they saw.
Questioning "What happened to the wave when the sound got bigger?"  ·  "Why do you think the picture changed?"  ·  "What do you think that picture is showing us?"
Explain 8 mins teacher demo
Sound is data
Open SoundWave Studio on the smartboard. Tap the blue button (Listen — ear icon). Before saying anything, point to the wave — it is flat. Then speak normally into the microphone and let pupils watch it react in real time (nothing is saved in Listen mode).

Do the same with a whisper, then a loud voice, then silence. Ask pupils what they notice each time.
  • Introduce microphone — it listens and turns sound into data the computer can store.
  • Introduce sound wave — the shape the computer draws to show us that data.
  • Introduce digital — stored on a device, not on paper or in the air.
Check for understanding "What happens to the wave when I stop talking?"  ·  "So what do you think a really loud sound would look like?"  ·  "Has anyone seen anything like this before — on a phone, a computer, anywhere?"
Explore 10 mins devices on
Have a go
Pupils put headphones on and open SoundWave Studio on the iPad. They tap the blue button (Listen) and watch the orange wave react to their own voice. Nothing is saved — they are just exploring.
  • Whisper a word. Watch the wave.
  • Say the same word at normal volume. Compare.
  • Stay silent for a few seconds. Watch it flatten.
  • Tap the blue button again to stop. Optional: tap Bigger on the wave for full-screen view on the board or iPad.
Stop the class briefly. Take two or three observations: "What did you find out about your wave?"
Talk prompt on display "The wave goes _______ when I _______."
Record 12 mins devices on
First recording
Model on the smartboard in SoundWave: tap record, say a short phrase clearly ("The lion roars across the savannah"), tap stop. Tap Yes, save it! Show the saved waveform in My Recordings. Tap ▶ Play.

Pupils each record one short phrase — it can be their name, a favourite animal, or a phrase from the Africa theme. They then:
  1. Tap stop, then Yes, save it!
  2. Look at the waveform in My Recordings.
  3. Tap ▶ Play and check it sounds clear.
  4. Optional: tap record again and save a second clip — compare the two waves in the list (loud vs quiet, or a second take).
Circulate and ask "Can you point to where you were speaking loudly on the wave?"  ·  "Does your wave look the same as your partner's? Why might it be different?"  ·  "Which recording is clearer — how can you tell from looking?"
Reflect 8 mins devices down
What did we find out?
Two or three pairs share their recording with the class on the smartboard (open their clip in SoundWave and play it, or replay from the teacher iPad). Point at features of the waveform together: "Can you see where they started speaking? Can you see a quiet gap?"

Return to the question from the hook: can a computer see sound? Take answers. Establish: yes — it turns sound into data and draws it as a wave. That wave tells us about the sound even before we press play.

Preview next lesson: "Next time you are going to record five parts of a poem — and start to arrange them in order."
Exit question (verbal — show of hands) "If I recorded a loud clap and a quiet whisper, which waveform would have bigger peaks?"  ·  "What does the word digital mean?"
Differentiation
Support
Pre-load SoundWave Studio on the iPad before the lesson; check microphone is allowed. Provide a printed sentence strip with a phrase ready to record. The adult focuses on the language: help pupils say "the wave goes up when I am loud" rather than just pointing.
Core
Record one phrase, view the waveform, play back. Encouraged to try a second recording and compare the two waves. Use talk prompts on display.
Stretch
Record two contrasting phrases (one loud, one quiet) and describe the difference in the waveforms. Begin thinking about which sounds they might want in their poem and why. Can they predict what the wave will look like before they record?
Assessment Opportunities
Observe
Can pupils describe what the waveform is showing? Do they notice it changes with volume? Do they make a connection between what they heard and what they see on screen?
Listen
During the reflect phase: can pupils explain in their own words that sound becomes data? Can they use any of the key vocabulary correctly and independently?
Working towards
Needs support to press record and can say the wave moves when there is sound.
Greater depth
Can describe the waveform using vocabulary (loud = bigger peaks, quiet = smaller) and begins to make predictions before recording.

Teacher note — SoundWave Studio (not Voice Memos) This unit uses one app throughout: SoundWave Studio on the smartboard and on pupil iPads. It matches the lesson visuals (orange wave, big red record button, child-friendly messages) and avoids switching between different apps.

Before the lesson: Upload the HTML file to your school site or open it from a shared link. Test on one iPad — tap Allow when asked for the microphone. Use ?lesson=2 on the URL (or the Open SoundWave link on the slides). Bookmarks on each iPad save time.

The focus is conceptual — pupils leave knowing that sound has a digital form that can be seen. Do not worry if recordings are unclear or short; that becomes the focus from Lesson 2 onwards. The most important moment is the live waveform demo: give it time and let pupils react before moving on.
